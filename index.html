Introduction to Entrepreneurship
Handout 01 · Foundations & Core Concepts
Entrepreneurship Core Concept
The process of creating, organizing, and operating a new business venture to generate profit while taking on financial risk. The word "entrepreneur" comes from the French word "entreprendre," meaning "to undertake."
Key Points
Entrepreneurs introduce new goods, services, or concepts to the market
They are willing to take on the risk of failure to achieve their goals
Opportunities for entrepreneurship have multiplied greatly in the modern era
Successful entrepreneurs must be curious about new knowledge and willing to explore the unknown
📌 Examples
Historical: Medieval traveling merchants and artisans who visited kingdoms to offer goods and services in exchange for payment.
Modern: A college student who identifies an unmet need in their community and launches a food delivery service using a bike and a social media page.
Digital Age: Mark Zuckerberg building Facebook from a dorm room — combining a new idea with risk-taking to generate profit.
Importance of Entrepreneurship Society Impact
Entrepreneurship plays a vital role in society through five key contributions that drive economic and social progress.
Key Points
Employment Creation: Hiring employees to assist with tasks reduces unemployment
Economic Development: Businesses drive growth by expanding operations and investing in technology
Technological Advancement: New ideas and products change how people live
Market Dynamics: New competition expands consumer options and lowers costs
Socio-Cultural Changes: Entrepreneurs tackle social issues like poverty, education, and healthcare
📌 Examples
Employment Creation: A new bakery opening in a town hires 5 bakers, 2 cashiers, and a delivery driver — reducing local unemployment.
Technological Advancement: Apple's creation of the iPhone transformed communication, media, and how millions access information globally.
Socio-Cultural Change: Bryan Benitez McClelland's Bambike manufactures bamboo bicycles, creating jobs while promoting eco-friendly living.
Types of Entrepreneurship Classification
There are 10 major types of entrepreneurship, each with unique characteristics and goals.
Key Points
Agripreneurship: Agricultural goods production and selling (crop cultivation, aquaculture)
Buyer Entrepreneurship: Using capital to acquire established businesses
Ecopreneurship: Green, environmentally friendly businesses
Imitator Entrepreneurship: Copying or adapting existing successful concepts
Intrapreneurship: Being an entrepreneur within an existing company
Large Business Entrepreneurship: Taking risks in large enterprises to remain competitive
Scalable Startup: Small enterprise designed to grow rapidly into a lucrative company
Small Business Entrepreneurship: Smaller scale with minimal employees
Social Entrepreneurship: Creating solutions to social issues (poverty, education, healthcare)
Technopreneurship: Combining technology with entrepreneurial skills
📌 Examples
Agripreneurship: Edita Aguinaldo Dacuycuy established the first organic dragon fruit farm in Northern Philippines.
Buyer Entrepreneurship: Tony Tan Caktiong acquired Greenwich, Chowking, Red Ribbon, and Mang Inasal alongside founding Jollibee.
Intrapreneurship: Paul Buchheit developed Gmail while working as Google's 23rd employee.
Scalable Startup: Roland Navarro de Ros founded Kumu, which grew to 7 million registered users.
Social Entrepreneurship: Bryan Benitez McClelland's Bambike creates jobs and promotes eco-friendly transportation.
SMMEs Enterprise Size
Small, Medium, and Micro-sized Enterprises classified by total capitalization. Any business activity engaged in industry, agribusiness, and services.
Key Points
Micro: Less than ₱50,000 capital
Cottage: ₱50,001 to ₱500,000 capital
Small: ₱500,001 to ₱5,000,000 capital
Medium: ₱5,000,001 to ₱20,000,000 capital
Total assets exclude the land where the business is situated
📌 Examples
Micro Enterprise: A sari-sari store in a barangay with a starting capital of ₱30,000 — classifies as a Micro enterprise.
Small Enterprise: A small restaurant with kitchen equipment and furniture totaling ₱1.5M — classifies as Small.
Medium Enterprise: A regional transport company with a fleet worth ₱10M — classifies as Medium.
Forms of Business Ownership Legal Structure
The legal structures available to entrepreneurs when establishing their businesses, each with distinct advantages and disadvantages.
Key Points
Sole Proprietorship: Single owner, easily created, unlimited personal liability, ends when owner dies
Partnership: Two or more co-owners, pooling of resources, unlimited liability solitarily
Corporation: Legal entity separate from stockholders, limited liability, costly and complex
Cooperative: Voluntary group with common interest, democratic control, tax privileges
📌 Examples
Sole Proprietorship: A freelance graphic designer operating under their own name with no partners.
Partnership: Two lawyers opening a joint law firm, sharing profits and responsibilities equally.
Corporation: Jollibee Foods Corporation — publicly listed, separate legal identity, stockholders have limited liability.
Cooperative: A farmers' cooperative in Davao that collectively markets and sells their produce to get better prices.
Entrepreneurial Competencies Skills
Core skills and characteristics that entrepreneurs need to make their businesses thrive in competitive markets.
Key Points
Leadership, Articulate, Sociable, Adaptable, Collaborative
Multifaceted, Resilient, Proactive, Innovative, Risk-taker
Creative and Passionate
Entrepreneurship is more accessible today due to technology and a supportive business environment
📌 Examples
Resilient: An entrepreneur whose first food stall failed during the pandemic pivots to online selling and eventually opens a successful cloud kitchen.
Innovative: Developing a mobile app that solves a specific problem faced by tricycle drivers in Davao City.
Proactive: A business owner who researches upcoming tax law changes and adjusts their accounting practices months in advance.
Motivation Theory Psychology
Theories explaining that entrepreneurial behavior is driven by purpose — an entrepreneur's drive to continue engaging in entrepreneurial activities.
Key Points
Extrinsic Motivation: Driven by external rewards (money, gifts, treats); fades when reward is absent
Intrinsic Motivation: Driven by passion and inner drive; includes achievement, power, and affiliation motives
Achievement motive: Strong desire to succeed and overcome difficulties
Power motive: Desire for control and influence over circumstances
Affiliation motive: Pursuing emotional connections with others
📌 Examples
Extrinsic: A salesperson who works harder only when monthly bonuses are offered — motivation drops when the bonus program ends.
Intrinsic (Achievement): An entrepreneur who wakes up early every day not for the money, but because they want to build the best bakery in their city.
Intrinsic (Affiliation): A social entrepreneur who starts a community kitchen primarily to connect with and help underprivileged families in their neighborhood.

Idea Generation
Handout 02 · Innovation & Opportunity
Idea Generation Core Concept
The creative process of generating new methods to solve problems and improve a product's or company's conditions, based on idea development, group discussions, and choosing the best alternative.
Key Points
Dreamer Stage: Ideas flow freely without fear of criticism, exploring new possibilities
Designer Stage: Analyzing ideas and exploring their feasibility
Detailer Stage: Identifying potential problems before executing the plan
Core elements: Entrepreneurial Mind Frame (optimism), Heart Flame (passion), Gut Game (intuition)
📌 Examples
Dreamer Stage: A student brainstorming during class — "What if we had an app that matched leftover food from restaurants with hungry students nearby?"
Designer Stage: Researching whether similar apps exist, checking technical requirements, and evaluating whether restaurant owners would participate.
Detailer Stage: Identifying problems like food safety regulations, delivery logistics, and how to handle perishable items before launch.
Opportunity Seeking Strategy
The process of identifying business opportunities through studying emerging trends, environmental changes, technological progress, government initiatives, people's interests, and personal experiences.
Key Points
Environmental changes: Physical, social, and business industry environments
Technological progress: Cutting-edge technology creates new business opportunities
Government initiatives: Policies and programs that inspire new ventures
People's interest: Hobbies and preferences can identify market demand
Experiences: Knowledge from working in specific fields
📌 Examples
Environmental Change: The COVID-19 pandemic caused a shift in demand — entrepreneurs identified the need for PPE supplies, online tutoring, and food delivery services.
Technology: The rise of smartphones created opportunities for app developers, mobile payment systems, and e-commerce platforms.
People's Interest: The growing interest in fitness and wellness created opportunities for gym studios, health food cafes, and fitness app developers.
Opportunity Screening — 12 Rs Evaluation
A framework of 12 criteria used to evaluate and filter business opportunities after the seeking phase.
Key Points
Relevance, Resonance, Reinforcement of Entrepreneurial Interests
Revenues, Responsiveness, Reach, Range
Revolutionary Impact, Returns, Relative Ease of Implementation
Resources Required, Risks
📌 Examples
Relevance Test: A passionate baker evaluates a cupcake delivery app idea — it aligns with their skills and the business's mission to deliver homemade goods.
Returns Test: Calculating whether selling handmade candles at ₱250 each, with only ₱50 cost per unit, offers enough return to justify the business.
Risks Test: Recognizing that opening a restaurant near 3 existing restaurants in the same area is high-risk before investing.
PESTEL Analysis Strategic Tool
An external environment analysis tool that identifies Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal forces that can positively or negatively impact a business.
Key Points
Political: Government policies, trade and taxation policies
Economic: Interest rates, unemployment rates, foreign exchange rates
Social: Demographics, cultural trends, lifestyle changes
Technological: Digital/mobile changes, automation, R&D
Environmental: Climate, waste disposal, carbon footprint, sustainability
Legal: Employment legislation, consumer law, health and safety regulations
📌 Examples
Political: A new law requiring all food businesses to have food safety certifications affects small carinderias — they must comply or face closure.
Economic: Rising inflation increases the cost of raw materials for bakers, forcing them to raise prices or reduce portion sizes.
Technological: The shift to cashless payments (GCash, Maya) means small stores must adopt digital payment terminals to stay competitive.
Environmental: Plastic bans implemented by LGUs force food packaging businesses to shift to biodegradable materials.
SWOT Analysis Strategic Tool
A strategic framework where Strengths and Weaknesses are internal factors, and Opportunities and Threats are external factors. Usually performed before the enterprise begins operations.
Key Points
Strengths: Internal advantages — strong brand, skilled workforce, unique capabilities
Weaknesses: Internal disadvantages — limited resources, inadequate market knowledge
Opportunities: External favorable conditions the business can leverage
Threats: External challenges — competitors, regulations, economic downturns
📌 Examples
Strength: A bakery with a secret family recipe that no competitor can replicate is a core internal strength.
Weakness: A startup with only ₱50,000 capital that needs ₱200,000 worth of equipment — limited financial resources.
Opportunity: A spike in health consciousness during a wellness campaign creates an opportunity for a salad bar startup.
Threat: A major fast-food chain opening a branch near a small local restaurant is an external competitive threat.
Types of Innovation Innovation
Three major types of business innovation that drive increased income and competitive advantage.
Key Points
Process Innovation: Change in how a product/service is manufactured, created, or distributed for greater efficiency
Product/Service Innovation: Creating new ideas/technologies to enhance customer value
Disruptive Innovation: Creates new markets or reshapes existing markets with a new value proposition
Low-end Disruptive: Products are too expensive relative to quality, new affordable options emerge
New-market Disruptive: Products restricted by features, new technology opens access to new markets
📌 Examples
Process Innovation: SM Supermarket's self-checkout lanes reduce queuing time and labor costs.
Product Innovation: Apple's iPhone (2007) combined a phone, camera, music player, and internet browser into one device.
Service Innovation: Netflix replaced physical DVD rentals with on-demand streaming available 24/7.
Low-end Disruption: Xiaomi and Oppo offered smartphones with high-end features at budget prices.
New-market Disruption: GoPro made action cameras accessible to everyday consumers, not just professional photographers.
Intellectual Property Legal
Intangible assets created by the mind that are protected by law, giving creators exclusive rights to their creations.
Key Points
Patent: Property right granted for an invention (design, process, improvement, or machine) by a government agency
Copyright: Exclusive right to use, copy, and duplicate original content granted to authors and creators
Trademark: Recognizable symbol, phrase, or logo that legally distinguishes a product from others
📌 Examples
Patent: Steve Jobs and three Apple coworkers filed for a personal computer patent in 1980, protecting their invention from competitors.
Copyright: A local author who writes a novel automatically holds copyright — no one can reproduce or sell the book without permission.
Trademark: The Coca-Cola logo (red and white design with distinctive font) is a registered trademark — competitors cannot use it.
Local Example: Jollibee's Jolly bee mascot and "Langhap Sarap" tagline are protected trademarks that distinguish them from other fast food chains.
Innovation Risks Risk Management
Seven categories of risk that businesses must monitor and manage when pursuing innovation.
Key Points
Economic Risk: Erratic economic changes decreasing sales, revenue, or profits
Financial Risk: Internal/external factors directly impacting a company's profits
Security/Fraud Risk: Cybersecurity threats from hackers and fraudsters
Compliance Risk: Legal penalties from violating laws and regulations
Human Risk: Employees' failure to carry out responsibilities
Reputation Risk: Negative impact on public opinion from lawsuits or bad reviews
Competitive Risk ("Comfort Risk"): Company becomes complacent and loses market share to rivals
📌 Examples
Economic Risk: Airlines lost 60% of revenue in 2020 due to COVID-19 travel restrictions.
Security/Fraud Risk: An e-commerce store gets hacked, exposing thousands of customers' payment information.
Reputation Risk: A single viral video showing poor food hygiene in a restaurant causes a massive drop in customers and revenue.
Competitive Risk: Nokia failed to innovate after dominating the mobile market, allowing Apple and Samsung to take over.

Marketing Aspects of New Ventures
Handout 03 · Market Research, Pricing & Strategy
Market Research Research
Research conducted with potential customers to determine the viability of a new product or service. Helps businesses decide whether to launch and how to market most effectively.
Key Points
Primary Research: Direct interaction with consumers — Quantitative (surveys, sampling) and Qualitative (interviews, focus groups)
Secondary Research: Uses existing data from public sources, commercial sources, company websites, and other published materials
Market Research Process: Define Problem → Buyer Persona → Questions/Research → List Competitors → Analyze → Report → Make Decisions
📌 Examples
Quantitative (Survey): A coffee shop owner distributes a Google Form to 200 customers asking them to rate service quality from 1–5.
Qualitative (Focus Group): A skincare brand invites 8 people to test a new moisturizer and records their reactions and feedback via video call.
Secondary Research: A restaurant owner reads government data on foot traffic in Davao's business districts before choosing a location.
Product Costing Finance
The method used to calculate the total cost of producing a product, comprising three main components: direct materials, direct labor, and overhead costs.
Key Points
Direct Materials: Raw materials used directly in production (wood, metal, plastic, chemicals)
Direct Labor: Wages, salaries, and benefits of employees involved in production
Overhead Costs: Indirect materials, indirect labor, machinery, rent, depreciation
Product Cost = Direct Materials + Direct Labor + Overhead Costs
Product Cost per Unit = Total Product Cost ÷ Number of Units Produced
📌 Examples
Mira Furniture Inc.: Wood (₱34,000) + Nails (₱6,500) + Paint (₱9,410) = ₱49,910 Direct Materials. Carpenters' wages (₱53,590.75) = Direct Labor. Security + Rent (₱23,000) = Overhead. Total = ₱126,500.75 ÷ 1,000 tables = ₱126.50/table.
Home Baking: Flour, butter, sugar, and eggs are direct materials. A baker's hourly wage is direct labor. The oven's electricity and kitchen rental are overhead costs.
Service Costing Finance
A method (also called operating costing) used to determine the cost of providing a specific service, identifying all costs related to resources used in delivering the service.
Key Points
Fixed Costs: Do not change regardless of activity level (rent, salaries, insurance)
Semi-variable Costs: Combination of fixed and variable (e.g., salaried + hourly labor)
Variable Costs: Change depending on output level (materials, commissions, packaging)
Total Service Cost = Fixed + Semi-Variable + Variable Costs
Service Cost per Unit = Total Service Cost ÷ Total Service Units
📌 Examples
Hotel Cleaning: La MiraBella Hotel: Salary ₱30,000 + Equipment ₱3,250 + Supplies ₱2,375 = ₱35,625 total. 30 days × 12 hours = 360 hours. Cost = ₱98.96/hour.
Bus Transport: Maya Transport: 5 buses × 40 seats × 40km × 30 days × 2 trips = 480,000 passenger-km. ₱98,000 ÷ 480,000 = ₱0.20/passenger-km.
Barbershop: Fixed costs (rent, electricity) + variable (supplies) + semi-variable (maintenance) determine the price per haircut.
Cost-Plus Pricing (Markup) Pricing
A pricing method where a predetermined markup percentage is added to the cost per unit to determine the appropriate selling price, ensuring all costs are covered and profit margins are achieved.
Key Points
Selling Price = Cost per unit × (1 + Markup Percentage)
Markup % = (Desired Profit ÷ Total Cost) × 100
Desired Profit = Desired Revenue − Total Cost
Ensures all costs are recovered while building in a planned profit margin
📌 Examples
Ice Cream: Edward's total product cost = ₱42,900 for 7,000 pieces. Desired revenue = ₱58,000. Markup = 35%. Cost per unit = ₱6.19. Selling price = ₱6.19 × 1.35 = ₱8.36/piece.
Barbershop: John's total service cost = ₱20,500 for 960 customers. Cost per customer = ₱21.35. With 95% markup, selling price = ₱21.35 × 1.95 = ₱41.63/customer.
General Rule: A clothing boutique that buys a dress for ₱500 and applies a 100% markup sells it for ₱1,000.
Pricing Strategies Marketing
Seven common pricing approaches used by start-up businesses to position products and services in the market.
Key Points
Premium Pricing: High price to create perception of high quality (luxury goods)
Economy Pricing: Low, affordable prices targeting cost-conscious consumers
Price Skimming: Start high, gradually lower as competitors enter the market
Penetration Pricing: Start low to capture market share, raise prices later
Psychological Pricing: Use prices like ₱99 instead of ₱100 to appear cheaper
Bundle Pricing: Multiple products offered together at a discounted price
Dynamic Pricing: Adjusts in real-time based on demand, competition, and customer behavior
📌 Examples
Premium: Gucci, Louis Vuitton, and Chanel charge high prices to maintain an exclusive, prestigious brand image.
Economy: Puregold and Alfamart compete on low prices to attract budget-conscious shoppers.
Price Skimming: Apple releases the iPhone at ₱70,000+, then older models drop to ₱30,000 when new ones launch.
Penetration: Netflix launched at a low monthly subscription to gain millions of users before gradually raising prices.
Dynamic: Grab and Angkas charge higher fares during rush hour or rainy nights due to high demand.
The Marketing Mix — 8Ps Marketing
A company's strategy for promoting its brand or product in the market, comprising eight interrelated components that must work together for marketing success.
Key Points
Product: The item offered — tangible (goods) or intangible (services/digital)
Price: The amount customers are willing to pay
Place: Where the product is purchased, sold, and experienced
Promotion: All activities used to communicate and sell (advertising, PR, direct marketing)
People: Key executives, endorsers, and the marketing team
Physical Evidence: Anything that helps customers trust the business (packaging, reviews, storefront)
Process: Steps used to solve customer problems and reach the target market
Positioning: How the business's brand is perceived relative to competitors
📌 Examples
Product: Jollibee's Chickenjoy is the tangible product; their loyalty app is an intangible digital offering.
Place: SM Mall branches vs. an online store on Lazada — both are different "places" for the same product.
Physical Evidence: A restaurant's clean interior, uniforms, and hygiene certificates on the wall build customer trust.
Positioning: Mang Inasal positions itself as the go-to affordable Filipino barbecue fast food against McDonald's and KFC.
Value Proposition & USP Positioning
A Value Proposition (VP) captures why a consumer should buy a product. A Unique Selling Proposition (USP) is a distinctive quality that sets a business apart from the competition.
Key Points
VP must be truthful and establish credibility with consumers
VP addresses customers' problems and fulfills their needs
USP highlights unique qualities and advantages through concise, memorable taglines
Branding creates a unique identity: logo, tagline, design, tone of voice, and packaging
📌 Examples
VP as Statement: Twitter — "See what's happening in the world right now." | Spotify — "Music for everyone." | Netflix — "See what's next."
USP: Jollibee's "Langhap Sarap" — the unique Filipino flavor and aroma that draws customers in before they even taste the food.
USP: Domino's Pizza — "We promise to deliver fresh, hot pizza in 30 minutes or less, or you get it for free!"
Branding: Coca-Cola's iconic red-and-white logo with a unique font has been recognizable worldwide for over 100 years.
Target Market & Customer Segmentation Marketing
A target market is a specific group of consumers a business wants to sell to. Customer segmentation divides the market into groups based on shared characteristics.
Key Points
Demographic: Age, gender, income, education, marital status
Geographic: Country, state, city, municipality, town
Psychographic: Personality, values, attitude, interests
Technographic: Mobile used, desktop used, apps and software used
Behavioral: Habits, tendencies, product usage frequency
Needs-based: Product must-haves for specific groups
Value-based: Economic value of a particular customer group
Firmographic: Based on shared organization attributes (B2B)
📌 Examples
Demographic Segmentation: A diaper brand targets mothers aged 20–35 with newborn to 3-year-old children.
Geographic Segmentation: A raincoat brand targets consumers in typhoon-prone regions like Eastern Visayas and Bicol.
Psychographic: A premium organic food brand targets health-conscious consumers who value sustainability and natural living.
Behavioral: A coffee shop app rewards customers who visit more than 3 times per week with loyalty points.

The Financial Aspect
Handout 04 · Financial Statements & Accounting
Start-up Costs Finance
Expenses a business incurs before its operations begin. Includes all bills and expenditures covered in preparation for the business's grand launch.
Key Points
One-time Expenses: Equipment, permits/licenses, technology, down payment, initial inventory, signage, furniture
Ongoing Expenses: Rent, accounting, taxes, insurance, payroll, utilities, marketing, loan payments
Start-up Assets: Long-term assets — inventory, computers, office equipment, furniture, vehicles
Assets ≠ Expenses: expenses are deductible against income; assets are not
Cash requirement = funds from investments/loans minus money spent on expenses and assets
📌 Examples
One-time: A food stall owner spends ₱15,000 on a business permit, ₱30,000 on a stainless steel cart, and ₱5,000 on initial food stock.
Ongoing: The same stall owner pays ₱3,000/month for market stall rental, ₱1,500 for electricity, and ₱500 for weekly supply restocking.
Asset vs. Expense: A ₱50,000 commercial oven is a fixed asset (not immediately deductible); a ₱500 bag of flour is an expense (deductible).
Capital Funding Strategies Finance
Models for raising the initial capital needed to start and operate a business, ranging from personal funds to institutional investors.
Key Points
Personal Investment: Investing personal financial instruments (stocks, bonds, real estate)
Love Money: Funds from family and friends ("patient capital" repaid as profits rise)
Venture Capital: Wealthy investors/banks provide financing in exchange for equity/stocks
Loans: Most common form — lender advances money, borrower agrees to repayment terms
Angels: Wealthy individuals who invest directly in small businesses in exchange for management oversight rights
Business Incubators: Share spaces and resources with established businesses at lower cost
Crowdfunding: Asking the general public for small donations, usually in exchange for equity
Grants/Subsidies: Government funds that don't require repayment; subsidies offset operating costs
📌 Examples
Love Money: A student borrows ₱20,000 from parents to start a small printing business with the promise to repay once profitable.
Venture Capital: A tech startup receives ₱5M from a venture capital firm in exchange for 20% equity in the company.
Crowdfunding: A Filipino artist raises ₱300,000 through a GoFundMe campaign to produce an indie film, rewarding backers with credits and merchandise.
Grant: DTI's DOLE-TUPAD or DSWD programs provide small capital grants to livelihood entrepreneurs without requiring repayment.
Income Statement Financial Statement
A financial statement that shows a company's revenues, expenses, and net profit over a specific period of time.
Key Points
Shows revenues minus expenses to arrive at net income (or net loss)
Helps business owners understand financial situation and make informed decisions about expenses
Provides stakeholders and shareholders insight into the business's financial state
Net income is carried to the Statement of Owner's Equity
Used alongside balance sheet and cash flow statement to calculate business taxes
📌 Examples
Simple Example: NetSolutions earned ₱7,500 in fees. Expenses: Wages ₱2,125 + Rent ₱800 + Supplies ₱800 + Utilities ₱450 + Misc ₱275 = ₱4,450. Net Income = ₱3,050.
Real-world Use: A restaurant owner reviews the monthly income statement and discovers food costs are too high at 45% of revenue — they adjust the menu to reduce waste.
Statement of Owner's Equity Financial Statement
A financial statement showing changes in the owner's equity during a specific period, including net income/loss, additional investments, and owner withdrawals.
Key Points
Shows beginning and ending balance of owner's equity
Includes net income or loss (from Income Statement), additional investments, and withdrawals/distributions
Helps stakeholders understand how equity has changed and what factors contributed
Retained profits can be reinvested in equipment, R&D, or other growth activities
Ending balance is carried to the Balance Sheet
📌 Examples
Simple Illustration: Beginning equity (Nov 1) = ₱0. Net Income = ₱3,050. Less Dividends = ₱2,000. Retained Earnings (Nov 30) = ₱1,050.
Business Scenario: A sole proprietor invested ₱50,000 in January, earned ₱80,000 profit during the year, and withdrew ₱30,000 for personal use. Ending equity = ₱100,000.
Balance Sheet Financial Statement
Shows a company's financial position at a specific point in time, providing a snapshot of assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity. Also known as the Statement of Financial Position.
Key Points
Assets: Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, property (current and non-current)
Liabilities: Accounts payable, loans, and other obligations (current and long-term)
Shareholders' Equity: Owner's investment and retained earnings
Shows a company's financial health at a specific time — typically end of fiscal year or month
Helps assess ability to pay bills and use credit to finance the business
📌 Examples
Simplified: Assets: Cash ₱590,000 + Supplies ₱5,500 + Land ₱2,000,000 = ₱2,645,000. Liabilities: Accounts Payable ₱40,000. Equity: Capital Stock ₱2,500,000 + Retained Earnings ₱105,000 = ₱2,605,000. Total = ₱2,645,000. ✓ Balanced.
Practical Use: A bank reviews a business's balance sheet before approving a loan — they check if assets exceed liabilities to gauge the business's solvency.
Statement of Cash Flow Financial Statement
Shows how cash is generated from operating activities, investing activities, and financing activities over a specific period.
Key Points
Operating Activities: Cash generated from delivering regular goods or services (revenue and expenses)
Investing Activities: Cash from purchasing or selling assets using free cash (real estate, vehicles, patents)
Financing Activities: Cash from both debt and equity financing
Reveals what phase a business is in — growing startup, mature company, or declining
Impacts internal decisions like budgeting and hiring
Final cash balance must match the Cash shown on the Balance Sheet
📌 Examples
NetSolutions Example: Operating: Cash received ₱7,500 − Expenses ₱4,600 = ₱2,900. Investing: Land purchase (₱2,000,000). Financing: Stock issued ₱2,500,000 − Dividends ₱2,000 = ₱2,300,000. Net Cash = ₱5,900.
Red Flag: An investor sees a company has positive net income but negative cash flow from operations — this signals potential accounting manipulation or cash collection problems.
Elements of Financial Statements Accounting
The five major categories that make up financial statements: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenue, and Expenses.
Key Points
Assets: Current (cash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses) and Fixed — Tangible (trucks, buildings) and Intangible (goodwill, patents, trademarks)
Liabilities: Current (accounts payable, salaries payable, notes payable) and Long-term (customer deposits, pension, long-term debt)
Equity: Owner's Capital, Withdrawals, Common Stocks, Retained Earnings
Revenue: Operating (sales, professional services, rental) and Non-operating (dividends, investment income, foreign exchange gains)
Expenses: Operating (cost of sales, utilities, advertising, depreciation) and Discretionary (travel, R&D, employee perks, office improvements)
📌 Examples
Current Asset: A convenience store's cash register money (₱10,000 in bills and coins) and unpaid customer invoices are current assets.
Intangible Asset: Jollibee's brand name (goodwill) and McDonald's golden arches logo (trademark) are valuable intangible assets.
Non-operating Revenue: A manufacturing company earns ₱50,000 in bank interest — this is non-operating revenue because it's not from their main business.
Discretionary Expense: A company's annual team-building retreat in Batangas is discretionary — it's non-essential but beneficial for morale.
The Accounting Equation Accounting
The fundamental element of the balance sheet: Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity. All financial transactions must maintain this balance.
Key Points
Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity (always must balance)
An increase in assets → corresponding increase in liability OR capital
An increase in one asset → decrease in another asset
A decrease in assets → decrease in liability OR decrease in capital
An increase in liability → corresponding decrease in capital
📌 Examples
Problem #1: Owner invests ₱10,000. Assets = ₱10,000 | Liabilities = ₱0 | Equity = ₱10,000. Balanced ✓
Problem #2: Business takes a ₱10,000 loan. Assets = ₱20,000 | Liabilities = ₱10,000 | Equity = ₱10,000. Balanced ✓
Problem #3: Business buys ₱4,000 in supplies on credit. Assets = ₱24,000 | Liabilities = ₱14,000 | Equity = ₱10,000. Balanced ✓
Daily Life Example: You buy a ₱5,000 laptop on credit card — your asset (laptop) increases by ₱5,000 AND your liability (credit card debt) increases by ₱5,000. Equation stays balanced.
