Introduction to Entrepreneurship
Handout 01 · Foundations, Types, Forms & Competencies
Entrepreneurship Core Concept
The word "entrepreneur" comes from the French word entreprendre, meaning "to undertake." During the Middle Ages, traveling merchants and artisans visited different kingdoms to offer goods and services. The modern definition emphasizes creating, organizing, and operating a new business venture to generate profit while taking on financial risk. The path is treacherous — full of surprising turns, obstacles, and dead ends — but the number of opportunities has exploded and multiplied.
Key Points
Entrepreneurs introduce new goods, services, or concepts to the market to make a profit
They are willing to take on the risk of failure to achieve their goals
In the past, entrepreneurs struggled due to limited understanding of product creation and inability to purchase raw materials in bulk
Successful entrepreneurs must be curious about new knowledge and willing to explore the unknown
The entrepreneurial journey involves risk, hard work, sacrifices, and setbacks
Owning a business provides flexible hours, independent decisions, and complete autonomy
📌 Examples
Historical: Medieval merchants traveling between kingdoms to trade specialized goods were the earliest entrepreneurs.
Modern: A college student who spots an unmet need for affordable tutoring and launches an online review center.
Digital Age: Mark Zuckerberg building Facebook from a dorm room — combining a new idea with risk-taking to generate massive profit.
Importance of Entrepreneurship in Society Society Impact
Entrepreneurship is one of the driving forces behind the development and improvement of society. Entrepreneurs influence the world and shape the future by producing everything from innovative ideas to jobs. There are five essential roles of entrepreneurship in society.
Key Points
Employment Creation: Entrepreneurs hire employees when launching businesses, increasing employment and reducing unemployment
Economic Development: Drives economic growth by expanding operations, investing in technology, and strengthening the workforce
Technological Advancement: Introduces new ideas and products — the rise of tech and the Internet created countless jobs and transformed communication
Market Dynamics: New competition expands consumer options and results in better goods at cheaper costs, encouraging existing businesses to improve
Socio-Cultural Changes: Entrepreneurs tackle social and cultural challenges by creating innovative, effective, and sustainable business solutions
📌 Examples
Employment: A new bakery opening hires bakers, cashiers, and delivery staff — reducing local unemployment.
Technology: Apple's iPhone transformed communication, media access, and daily life globally.
Socio-Cultural: Bambike's Bryan McClelland creates jobs while advocating eco-friendly bamboo transportation.
Types of Entrepreneurship Classification
Based on Rebecca David's Entrepreneurship 101 (2022), there are 10 major types of entrepreneurship, each with unique characteristics and goals.
Key Points
Agripreneurship: Production and selling of agricultural goods — crop cultivation, aquaculture, agro-bio products, biotechnical services
Buyer Entrepreneurship: Uses capital to acquire established companies or merge with smaller businesses that already have a brand and revenue
Ecopreneurship: Creating "green" businesses to save the Earth while making profit
Imitator Entrepreneurship: Copying or adapting an existing successful business concept or product
Intrapreneurship: Acting as an entrepreneur within an existing company — taking risks, innovating, and developing new ideas for the company
Large Business Entrepreneurship: Taking calculated risks in large enterprises to develop new market offerings and respond to changing markets
Scalable Startup Entrepreneurship: Launching a small enterprise designed to quickly expand into a highly lucrative company; focused on disruptive innovation
Small Business Entrepreneurship: Starts on a smaller scale, minimal employees, easy to manage; sari-sari stores are among the most common in the Philippines
Social Entrepreneurship: Innovating solutions to pressing social issues — poverty, education, healthcare
Technopreneurship: Combining entrepreneurial skills and technology to solve complex business problems

Social: Bryan Benitez McClelland's Bambike provides poverty alleviation through handmade bamboo bicycles in partnership with GawadKalinga.

Entrepreneurial Competencies Skills
Successful business people have specific characteristics and skills that help them make it big. Possessing a wide range of essential entrepreneurial skills is paramount — comprehending risks and rewards, and devising strategies for contingencies.
Key Points
Leadership: Display passion, vision, and mission in every interaction with investors, employees, and external stakeholders
Articulate: Communication is vital for closing sales, boosting morale, resolving conflicts, and negotiating contracts
Sociable: Aligning the organizational approach with the unique characteristics of the business and its employees
Adaptable: Must be decisive, passionate, adaptable, and willing to make changes
Collaborative: Connecting with managers, investors, partners, and stakeholders as equals; valuing each person's contribution
Multifaceted: Adaptable and multi-skilled — managing all business aspects including sales, marketing, and stakeholder management
Resilient: Embraces failure and never gives up; views setbacks as chances to gain wisdom and advance
Proactive: Takes initiative to address problems before they happen — finances, new markets, or benchmarking competitors
Innovative: Grows the business and satisfies customers by developing new market offerings that meet changing demands
Risk-taker: Aware of potential risks and proactive in mitigating them
Creative: Relentless curiosity to explore novel concepts and prospects that evolve into profitable ventures
Passion: Constant source of motivation driving entrepreneurs to revolutionize outdated methods and introduce innovative concepts
📌 Examples
Resilient: An entrepreneur whose first food stall failed during the pandemic pivots to online selling, eventually opening a successful cloud kitchen.
Proactive: A business owner researches upcoming tax regulation changes months in advance and adjusts accounting practices before they take effect.
Innovative: Developing a mobile app that solves a specific problem faced by tricycle drivers in Davao City.
Motivation Theory Psychology
Motivation theories suggest that behavior is not random but driven by purpose to achieve specific objectives. Entrepreneurial motivation refers to an entrepreneur's drive to continue engaging in entrepreneurial activities — which can encompass taking risks, identifying new opportunities, and pursuing innovation.
Key Points
Extrinsic Motivation: Motivating individuals by offering external rewards such as money, gifts, or treats; fades when the reward is absent or not at par with their interest
Intrinsic Motivation: People do the task because they are passionate; includes three categories:
Achievement Motive: Strong motivation to succeed, achieve greatness, and conquer difficulties in the quest for objectives
Power Motive: Desire for control, influence, and the ability to impact others and change circumstances
Affiliation Motive: The act of pursuing, upholding, or revitalizing an emotional connection with others
📌 Examples
Extrinsic: A salesperson works harder only when bonuses are offered — motivation drops once the bonus program ends.
Achievement Motive: An entrepreneur who wakes up early every day not for money, but because they want to build the best bakery in their city.
Affiliation Motive: A social entrepreneur starts a community kitchen primarily to build meaningful connections with underprivileged families.
Power Motive: An entrepreneur who expands their business into multiple cities driven by a desire to lead and shape their industry.

Idea Generation Core Concept
The creative process of generating new methods to solve problems and improve a product's or company's conditions. It is based on idea development, group discussions, choosing the best alternative, and implementing the idea in real-world scenarios. The idea does not need to be practical — it can also be a mere thought.
3 Crucial Stages of Identifying a Good Idea (McCreanor, 2023)
Dreamer Stage: Ideas flow freely without fear of criticism or judgment; encourages exploring new possibilities and breaking free from conventional thinking
Designer Stage: Provides an opportunity to analyze ideas and explore their feasibility
Detailer Stage: Potential problems are thoroughly identified; crucial for ensuring all aspects are addressed before executing — saves time, money, and resources
Core of Entrepreneurial Ideas
Entrepreneurial Mind Frame: Equips the entrepreneur to be optimistic during a crisis; even knowing businesses are risky, they don't get easily discouraged
Entrepreneurial Heart Flame: Shows the passion of an entrepreneur in fulfilling their vision and mission; they devote themselves to their venture
Entrepreneurial Gut Game: Exhibits an entrepreneur's intuitive side — a good sense of whether something will work without logical, systematic thinking; brings out confidence and courage
Ways to Generate Ideas
Imitate the successful ideas of others; address existing problems; transform waste through recycling
Pay attention to business professionals, prospective customers, rivals, and collaborators
Daydream and allow imagination to take over; transform a hobby into a profitable venture
Improve a product by maximizing advantages and overcoming its limitations; make new connections outside the normal circle
📌 Examples
Dreamer Stage: "What if restaurants gave unsold food to nearby students instead of throwing it away?" — freely imagined, no judgment.
Designer Stage: Researching similar apps, checking food safety regulations, and evaluating whether restaurants would participate.
Detailer Stage: Identifying problems like delivery logistics, liability for spoiled food, and legal permits before proceeding.
Gut Game: A seasoned street food vendor "feels" that sisig tacos will sell in BGC without conducting formal research — and they're right.
Opportunity Seeking Strategy
Entrepreneurs are known for being curious and innovative — their minds always seek to discover new things or ideas and see whether they will work in the marketplace. They create value by introducing the latest market offerings or finding new solutions to existing problems. There are five main sources of opportunity.
Key Points
Environmental Changes: Can occur naturally or from human activities (climate change, deforestation, pollution, biodiversity loss); includes physical environment (climate, natural resources, wildlife), social environment (Political, Economic, Sociocultural, Technological factors), and business industry environment (competitors, customers, creditors, employees, government, suppliers)
Technological Progress: Enables exploration of countless business opportunities and groundbreaking discoveries using cutting-edge technology
Government's Initiatives and Policies: Government priorities, projects, programs, and policies provide valuable sources of inspiration
People's Interest: Knowing interests and hobbies can inspire entrepreneurial ideas and identify areas of market demand
Experiences: Knowledge and abilities acquired by working in a specific field hold immense promise for creating integrated commercial enterprises
📌 Examples
Environmental Change: The COVID-19 pandemic triggered demand for PPE supplies, online tutoring services, and food delivery apps.
Technology: The proliferation of smartphones created opportunities for app developers, mobile payment companies, and e-commerce platforms.
Government: DOLE's livelihood programs inspire unemployed individuals to start small enterprises with government support.
People's Interest: The growing wellness trend created opportunities for gym studios, healthy food cafes, and fitness app developers.
Opportunity Screening — The 12 Rs Evaluation
After searching for opportunities, the subsequent phase is to screen those opportunities. Entrepreneurs must be cautious when selecting and analyzing potential opportunities to significantly enhance their chances of success.
The 12 Rs of Opportunity Screening
Relevance: The opportunity should align perfectly with the business's vision, mission, and goals
Resonance: Should be harmonious with the values and virtues the entrepreneur desires for the business
Reinforcement of Entrepreneurial Interests: Should match the entrepreneur's interests, talents, and skills
Revenues: Verify if products/services possess a market with substantial potential for growth and sales
Responsiveness: The opportunity should effectively meet the needs and desires of customers
Reach: Opportunities to grow the business through branches, franchises, distributors, and dealerships
Range: Opportunities to create a wide range of products and services for diverse markets
Revolutionary Impact: The entrepreneur must visualize the business as a revolutionary force of the future
Returns: Should offer a low initial investment with potential for substantial returns at a higher selling price
Relative Ease of Implementation: Should be simple to seize with few obstacles to overcome
Resources Required: Should require fewer resources
Risks: Entrepreneurs should recognize high-risk opportunities with clarity and confidence
📌 Examples
Relevance: A baker evaluates a cupcake delivery app idea — it aligns perfectly with their skills and business mission.
Returns: Handmade candles costing ₱50 each but selling for ₱250 offer excellent returns on a modest investment.
Risks: Opening a restaurant next to 3 existing ones in a small community is identified as a high-risk opportunity — screened out.
Opportunity Seizing — External Environment Tools Strategy
Seizing opportunities is the ultimate step in planning for a successful business venture. Entrepreneurs learn from both successes and failures of other businesses to identify key factors for success. Three key analytical tools assist with this.
A. PESTEL Analysis
Political: Government and its policies — trade, fiscal, and taxation policies
Economic: Impact of the economy — interest rates, employment/unemployment rates, raw material costs, foreign exchange rates
Social: Emerging trends — changing family demographics, education levels, cultural trends, attitudes, lifestyle changes
Technological: Digital/mobile technology changes, automation, research and development
Legal: Employment legislation, consumer law, health and safety, international and trade regulations
Environmental: Climate, recycling procedures, carbon footprint, waste disposal, sustainability
B. Competitor Analysis
Classify competitors into direct and indirect groups; analyze products/services offered, market share, marketing strategies, strengths/weaknesses, threats, and opportunities they present
C. SWOT Analysis (performed before operations begin)
Strengths (Internal): Competitive advantage factors — strong brand, skilled workforce, superior product, extensive distribution, unique technology
Weaknesses (Internal): Disadvantages — inadequate market knowledge, limited financial resources, deficiencies in infrastructure, ineffective marketing, lack of technological expertise
Opportunities (External): Favorable external conditions or trends the organization can leverage
Threats (External): Challenges — competitors, changing market trends, new regulations, economic conditions, technological advancements, natural disasters
📌 Examples
PESTEL — Political: A new food safety law requiring all food businesses to obtain FDA certification forces small carinderias to comply or face closure.
PESTEL — Economic: Rising inflation increases raw material costs for bakers, forcing them to raise product prices.
SWOT — Strength: A bakery with an exclusive family recipe that no competitor can replicate.
SWOT — Threat: A major fast-food chain opening a branch near a small local restaurant is an external competitive threat.
Types of Innovation Innovation
Although "business innovation" is often used to describe technology, the real meaning is increasing income. All industries are disrupted by technology, so companies must innovate to stay current. The main benefit is increased business income and an enhanced bottom line. There are three major types.
Key Points
Process Innovation: A change in how a product or service is manufactured, created, or distributed to achieve greater efficiency
Product or Service Innovation: Creating and implementing new ideas, concepts, or technologies to enhance customer value and differentiate market offerings
Disruptive Innovation: Introduces a new value proposition — either creates new markets or reshapes existing ones
Low-end Disruptive Innovation: Occurs when products/services are too expensive relative to their quality — new affordable alternatives emerge
New-market Disruptive Innovation: Occurs when product features restrict accessibility or confine usage to inconvenient, centralized venues
📌 Examples
Process Innovation: SM, Rustan's, and Robinson's implemented self-checkout systems allowing customers to scan and pay independently. Robotic automation in Tesla and Apple factories streamlines production. Online booking via Agoda and Booking.com eliminated the need for travel agents.
Product Innovation: Apple's iPhone (2007) combined multiple devices into one. Plant-based meat alternatives (Beyond Meat, Impossible Foods) mimic real meat from plant-based ingredients.
Service Innovation: Uber, Lyft, and Grab disrupted traditional taxis with ride-sharing. Netflix transformed media consumption from physical DVDs to on-demand streaming.
Low-end Disruption: Southwest Airlines and Ryanair offered budget flights without extra services. Xiaomi and Oppo introduced affordable smartphones with flagship features.
New-market Disruption: GoPro made action cameras accessible to everyday consumers. WhatsApp disrupted telecommunications with free messaging.
Risks Involved in Innovation Risk Management
According to McCreanor (2023), there are seven risks that a business should watch out for when innovating.
Key Points
Economic Risk: Erratic economic changes that decrease sales, revenue, or profits — e.g., the 60% airline revenue drop in 2020 due to COVID-19; constantly present due to global crises
Financial Risk: Internal and external circumstances that directly impact a company's profits — defaults on loans, market changes, or losses
Security and Fraud Risk: Growth of online businesses brings increased risk from hackers, cybercriminals, and fraudsters exploiting e-commerce platforms
Compliance Risk: Potential for legal penalties, financial loss, and material consequences from violating external laws and regulations or internal standards
Human Risk: Employees' failure or inability to carry out essential responsibilities — caused by alcohol/drug abuse, theft, or uncontrollable health issues
Risk of Reputation: Lawsuits, negative social media reviews, or other occurrences that significantly impact public opinion, harming a business's reputation and overall success
Competitive Risk ("Comfort Risk"): Arises when a company becomes so complacent with its success that it neglects to consistently enhance its products — making it easier for rivals to gain market share
📌 Examples
Economic Risk: Airlines lost 60% of revenue in 2020 due to COVID-19 travel restrictions.
Security/Fraud: An e-commerce store gets hacked, exposing thousands of customer payment details.
Reputation: A single viral video showing poor hygiene causes a massive drop in restaurant customers.
Competitive (Comfort): Nokia failed to innovate after dominating the mobile market, allowing Apple and Samsung to take over entirely.
Intellectual Property Legal
Various forms of intangible assets that fall under the umbrella of intellectual property. These are creations of the mind protected by law, granting creators exclusive rights.
Key Points
Patent: A property right granted to an invention by a government agency (e.g., U.S. Patent and Trademark Office). The invention — a design, process, improvement, or physical machine — is granted exclusive rights. Innovation and programming businesses frequently use patents for their plans
Copyrights: The exclusive right to use, copy, and duplicate original content granted to authors and creators. Protects authors of books and musicians. Copyright allows original creators to let others use the work by signing a license agreement
Trademarks: A recognizable symbol, phrase, or logo that legally distinguishes a product from others. Exclusively assigned to one company. Frequently associated with a company's brand identity
📌 Examples
Patent: Steve Jobs and three Apple Inc. coworkers filed for a personal computer patent in 1980, giving Apple exclusive rights to the design.
Copyright: A local author who writes a novel holds automatic copyright — no publisher can reproduce or sell it without permission.
Trademark: The Coca-Cola logo (red and white with distinctive font) is a registered trademark — competitors cannot copy or use it.
Local trademark: Jollibee's Jolly Bee mascot and "Langhap Sarap" tagline are protected trademarks distinguishing them from other fast food brands.

Market Research Research
Market research is crucial to understanding the potential success of a new product or service. It involves conducting research with potential customers to determine viability and helps businesses decide whether to launch and how to market it best.
Two Types of Market Research
Primary Research: Direct interaction with consumers or third-party entities to gather data
Quantitative Research: Analyzes numerical data using statistical methods; common methods include Customer Surveys (yes/no or 1–5 rating scale), Binary or Rating Scale Questionnaires, and Sampling (minimizes bias using random/probability sampling)
Qualitative Research: Provides insight into underlying reasons and motivations; common methods: Focus Group Discussion (6–10 individuals via online surveys), Individual Interviews (personal interaction with structured questions), Ethnographic Research (observing consumers in their actual homes/environments), Case Study Research (explores specific issue in real-life context), Open-ended Questionnaires (extended responses beyond yes/no)
Secondary Research: Uses data and insights from other sources — Public Sources (government departments, public libraries — free), Commercial Sources (newspapers, magazines, television, journals), Company Websites, and Other Sources (published market studies, analyst reports, customer feedback, recorded meetings)
7-Step Market Research Process (McCreanor, 2023)
Step 1: Define the Problem — identify and define a specific problem or objective
Step 2: Define Buyer Persona — create a fictional representation of the ideal customer
Step 3: Prepare Questions and Conduct Research — use simple language; avoid ambiguous terms
Step 4: List Major Competitors — include strengths, weaknesses, financial situations, and reputations
Step 5: Analyze the Results — quantitative data includes age, profession, gender; qualitative data (experiences, emotions) is separate
Step 6: Develop a Research Report — share insights with team, marketing managers, and executives
Step 7: Make Decisions — based on carefully considering all information; once decided, carry out per planned course
📌 Examples
Quantitative Survey: A coffee shop owner sends 200 customers a Google Form asking them to rate service quality from 1–5.
Focus Group: A skincare brand invites 8 people to test a new moisturizer via video call and records their reactions.
Secondary Research: A restaurant owner reads government foot traffic data for Davao's business districts before choosing a location.
Ethnographic: A baby product company observes parents in their homes using feeding bottles to discover design frustrations.
Product and Service Development (PSD) Development
PSD encompasses much more than just production — it involves a holistic approach including research, design, testing, and improving the product and service. It goes beyond manufacturing to understand customer needs, market trends, and technological advancements.
Two Types of Customer Requirements
Service Requirement: Intangible — timely delivery, friendly customer service, convenient payment options; how customers expect to be treated
Output Requirements (Goods): Physical objects — features, performance, quality, reliability, durability, size, weight, compatibility, user-friendliness; must meet industry standards, have good value for money, and offer warranty/after-sales support
6-Step PSD Process
Step 1: Trend Analysis and Real-world Observations — identify product/service opportunity gaps combined with emerging trends
Step 2: Consumer and Market Research — identify needs, desires, and what features will satisfy the target market
Step 3: Actionable Insights — brainstorming and creative thinking to generate new ideas and improve existing offerings
Step 4: Concept Development and Refinement — Ideation (evaluate promising ideas) → Prototyping (build a model) → Testing (verify function and appeal) → Refinement (use feedback to craft the final version)
Step 5: Implementation and Launch — after market testing and adjustments, the final product/service is officially released
Step 6: Result Assessment — gather customer input, analyze sales data, and consider achievement indicators for future improvement
PSD Flowchart
A flowchart is a visual representation of a process or system using symbols connected by arrows — shows steps, decisions, and flow of information
Correct flowchart symbols facilitate clear communication, enhance readability, and minimize misinterpretation
Common symbols: Terminator (start/end), Process (rectangle), Decision (diamond), Data (parallelogram), Connector, Subroutine
📌 Examples
Prototyping: Before launching a new drink flavor, a beverage company produces 500 sample bottles and distributes them in a shopping mall for free tasting.
Refinement: After testing reveals customers find a new app confusing, the UI is redesigned before official launch.
Result Assessment: After launching a new product, sales data for the first 3 months is analyzed to determine whether to continue or adjust.
Product Costing Finance
Product cost is the cost associated with making a product to be sold. It consists of three main components: direct material, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead — essential in determining the total expenses of producing a product.
Key Points
Direct Materials: Raw materials or components used directly in production — examples: wood, metal, plastic, fabric, chemicals
Direct Labor: Cost of labor involved in producing a product — includes wages, salaries, and benefits of employees
Overhead Costs: Expenses related to production — indirect materials (glue, oil, tape, cleaning supplies), indirect labor (security guards, drivers, legal advisers), and other overheads (factory/equipment depreciation, insurance, property taxes, rent/lease, utilities)
Product Cost = Direct Materials + Direct Labor + Overhead Costs
Product Cost per Unit = Total Product Cost ÷ Number of Units Produced
📌 Examples
Mira Furniture (1,000 tables): Woods ₱34,000 + Nails ₱6,500 + Paint ₱9,410 = ₱49,910 (DM). Carpenters' wages ₱53,590.75 (DL). Security ₱8,000 + Rent ₱15,000 = ₱23,000 (OH). Total = ₱126,500.75 ÷ 1,000 = ₱126.50/table.
Edward's Ice Cream (7,000 pieces): Milk ₱4,000 + Preservatives ₱3,000 + Freezer ₱12,000 = ₱19,000 (DM). Labor ₱500 × 28 = ₱14,000 (DL). Electricity ₱900 + Rent ₱9,000 = ₱9,900 (OH). Total = ₱42,900 ÷ 7,000 = ₱6.19/piece.
Service Costing Finance
Service costing (also called operating costing) is a method used to determine the cost of providing a specific service. It involves identifying all costs related to the resources used in delivering the service. Commonly used in hotels, IT, accounting firms, healthcare, advertising agencies, banks, educational institutions, engineering companies, law firms, restaurants, logistics, and transport.
Key Points
Fixed Costs: Do not change regardless of activity level — rent, lease payments, salaries, utility bills, insurance, loan repayments
Semi-variable Costs: Both fixed and variable — labor is often divided: fixed monthly salaried employees + variable hourly employees
Variable Costs: Change depending on output — materials, productivity-based labor, production supplies, commissions, delivery charges, packaging, credit card fees
Unit of Measurement: Varies by service type — kiloliter (water), per meal/person (canteen), passenger-km (transport), room days (hotel), kilowatt-hour (electricity), tons-km (logistics), per patient (hospital)
Total Service Cost = Fixed + Semi-Variable + Variable Costs
Service Cost per Unit = Total Service Cost ÷ Total Service Unit
📌 Examples
La MiraBella Hotel (cleaning): Salary ₱30,000 + Equipment ₱3,250 (Fixed) + Supplies ₱2,375 (Variable) = ₱35,625 total. 30 days × 12 hours = 360 hours. Cost = ₱35,625 ÷ 360 = ₱98.96/hour.
Maya Transport (buses): Fixed ₱50,000 + Variable ₱40,000 + Semi-variable ₱8,000 = ₱98,000. 5 buses × 40 seats × 40km × 30 days × 2 trips = 480,000 passenger-km. Cost = ₱98,000 ÷ 480,000 = ₱0.20/passenger-km.
Pricing Strategies Marketing
Price is a crucial factor in influencing consumer choice. Thorough analysis of the business's nature, market competition, brand positioning, and target market is required. Two key pricing concepts: Cost-Plus Pricing (Markup) and the Seven Common Pricing Strategies.
Cost-Plus Pricing (Markup)
Selling Price = Cost per unit × (1 + Markup Percentage)
Markup % = (Desired Profit ÷ Total Cost) × 100
Desired Profit = Desired Revenue − Total Cost
Objectives of Pricing
Competition-Based: Industry competition level determines the pricing strategy
Cost-Based: Price determined by considering the total cost involved in production
Customer-Value: Price determined by the value or benefits to customers
Market Share: Centered around expanding market share and cultivating customer awareness and loyalty
Sales Orientation: Designed to boost sales volume and maximize profits within a specific timeframe
Customer-Driven: Price established depending on how much the customer is willing to pay
7 Most Common Pricing Strategies
Premium Pricing: High price point to create perception of higher quality — used for luxury brands or unique products
Economy Pricing: Relatively low and affordable — targets customers who want to save money
Price Skimming: Initially set high to generate maximum interest; lowered as competitors enter the market
Penetration Pricing: Initially set low to quickly enter a competitive market, gain customer base, then increase later
Psychological Pricing: Uses specific price points (₱99 instead of ₱100) to influence consumer perception
Bundle Pricing: Multiple products/services offered together at a discounted price — creates illusion of a good deal
Dynamic Pricing: Adjusts in real-time using algorithms based on demand, competition, and customer behavior
📌 Examples
Premium: Gucci, Louis Vuitton, and Chanel maintain high prices to project exclusivity and prestige.
Economy: Puregold, Alfamart, Cebu Pacific, and AirAsia maximize profits through high volume at low prices.
Price Skimming: Apple releases each new iPhone at a premium then reduces prices of older models when new ones launch.
Penetration: Netflix started at low subscription rates to build a massive user base before gradually raising prices.
Psychological: Beauty salons price haircuts at ₱299 rather than ₱300 to appear meaningfully cheaper.
Dynamic: Grab, Foodpanda, Joyride, and Angkas charge surge pricing during peak hours or bad weather.
Marketing Strategies — Target Market & The 8Ps Marketing
A target market is a specific group of consumers a business wants to sell to — a clearly defined segment based on demographics, psychographics, behaviors, and needs. The Marketing Mix (8Ps) is a company's strategy for promoting its brand in the market.
Customer Segmentation Models
Demographic: Age, gender, income, education, marital status
Geographic: Country, state, city, municipality, town
Psychographic: Personality, values, attitude, interest
Technographic: Mobile used, desktop, apps and software used
Behavioral: Tendencies, habits, features or product use
Needs-based: Product/service must-haves, needs of specific groups
Value-based: Economic value of a particular customer group
Firmographic: Group customers based on shared organization attributes (B2B)
The 8Ps of Marketing
1. Product: Tangible (physical goods) or intangible (digital goods or services) offered to customers
2. Price: Amount customers are willing to pay for a product or service
3. Place: Location of purchase, sale, and experience — varies between retail and direct online sales
4. Promotion: Entire set of activities used to promote — advertising, personal selling, direct marketing, sales promotion, public relations
5. People: Key executives, endorsers, and the marketing team — accountable for delivering marketing promises
6. Physical Evidence: Anything that helps the target audience trust the business — packaging, storefront, reviews, testimonials
7. Process: Steps that help businesses figure out customer problems, look at market opportunities, and create marketing materials
8. Positioning: Capacity to alter how consumers perceive a brand compared to competitors
Promotions Mix
Advertising: Mass communication — Print (newspapers, magazines), Broadcast (TV, radio, podcasts), Public/Outdoor (billboards, transit ads), Digital (online display ads, SEO, social media)
Selling (Personal Selling): Face-to-face selling using interpersonal skills — retail salespersons, real estate agents, insurance agents, medical representatives
Direct Marketing: Communicates directly to individual customers — email, direct mail, telemarketing, text blast, leaflet distribution, social media marketing
Sales Promotion: Short-term campaigns — product bundles, flash sales, free trials, free shipping, Buy-1-Take-1, coupons, vouchers
Public Relations: Managing how information is disseminated to the public — product placement, sponsorship, brand ambassadors, corporate social responsibility, social media campaigns
Media Framework
Paid Media: Public relations, marketing, and promotional activities companies create and pay for themselves
Earned Media: News articles, blogs, and social network discussions mentioning the brand — provides word-of-mouth and PR benefits without direct payment
Owned Media: Online platforms the business possesses and controls — blog, website, social media channels
📌 Examples
Demographic: A diaper brand targets mothers aged 20–35 with children aged 0–3.
Dynamic Positioning: Mang Inasal positions itself as the affordable Filipino BBQ fast food alternative to McDonald's and KFC.
Earned Media: A food blogger posts a glowing review of a new restaurant without being paid — this is earned media for the restaurant.
Sales Promotion: "Buy 2 Get 1 Free" at a local bakery during its anniversary week is a short-term sales promotion campaign.
Value Proposition, USP & Branding Positioning
Part of the 8th P (Positioning). A Value Proposition (VP) captures why a consumer should purchase a company's product or service. A Unique Selling Proposition (USP) is a distinctive quality that sets the business apart from the competition. Branding creates a unique identity for the company.
Key Points
VP must be built on truthful foundations to establish credibility; addresses customers' problems and fulfills their needs
VP as a function: Example — Donna's Food Store open 24/7 near a call center addresses a real need
VP as a statement: Twitter — "See what's happening in the world right now." | Spotify — "Music for everyone." | Netflix — "See what's next." | AirBnB — "Book unique homes and experiences." | Bitly — "Short links, Big results."
USP highlights specific unique qualities and advantages; be specific and create a concise tagline that sticks in people's minds
Branding elements: logo, tagline, attractive design, tone of voice, social media captions, billboard colors, packaging materials
Ideal packaging: visually appealing, affordable, offers excellent product protection, provides helpful information, enables convenient use and promotion
📌 Examples
USP: Jollibee's "Langhap Sarap" — the unique Filipino flavor and aroma draws customers in before they even taste the food.
USP: Domino's Pizza — "We promise to deliver fresh, hot pizza in 30 minutes or less, or you get it for free!"
Branding: Coca-Cola's iconic red-and-white logo with a unique font has captivated buyers for over a hundred years.
Branding: Ferrari presents itself as a luxurious status symbol; Eveready claims the most reliable and durable batteries; Mang Inasal positions as affordable BBQ chicken fast food.
Facility Management, Layout & Business Location Operations
Facilities Management coordinates people, places, and processes to care for a business's structures, buildings, equipment, and physical needs to improve productivity. Regular inspections and maintenance are necessary to avoid costly repairs.
Categories of Facilities Management
Hard Services: Physical modifications to a building — water sprinklers, fire alarm systems, fire extinguishers, CCTV cameras, theft alarms, electrical systems, air-conditioning systems
Soft Services: Enhance the workplace environment — landscaping, event support, cleaning, canteen/pantry management, pest control
Business Location Factors
Controllable: Proximity to market, supply of materials, transportation facilities (air, rail, water, roads, pipelines), infrastructure availability, labor wages and governing laws
Non-controllable: Government policies (labor laws, building codes, safety, taxes, data privacy), climate and environmental conditions, supporting industries (supplier proximity)
Workplace Structures
Virtual Office: Enhances efficiency, reduces commuting and overhead costs, increases flexibility; streamlines data accessibility and backup
Physical Office — Open Design: Minimal walls/barriers; promotes collaboration and communication
Physical Office — Cellular Design: Private, individual "cell" workspaces or enclosed cubicles for each employee
Physical Office — Co-Working Space: Open work areas with shared desks; promotes collaboration and flexibility
Physical Office — Combination Design: Tailored to specific organizational needs — dynamic and versatile
Manufacturing/Plant Layouts
Product/Line Layout: One product type per area, produced in large standardized quantities along one continuous line
Process/Functional Layout: Machinery grouped by type of operation — best for low production needs
Fixed Position Layout: Products built in one fixed location; materials and equipment come to the product — used when products are too heavy to move
Combination Layout: Mix of other layouts — used when items have different types and sizes
📌 Examples
Hard Services: Installing a sprinkler system and CCTV network in a new commercial building before tenants move in.
Virtual Office: A freelance digital marketing team collaborates entirely via Zoom, Slack, and Google Drive — no physical office needed.
Fixed Position Layout: Shipbuilding — the ship stays in the dry dock while workers and heavy equipment come to the vessel.
Controllable Factor: A perishable goods supplier chooses a location near the central market to ensure fast delivery and reduce spoilage.

Start-up Costs & Capital Funding Finance
Starting a business requires careful planning to anticipate and prevent financial challenges. Start-up costs are expenses a business incurs before operations begin. Every business has unique costs; businesses typically fall into three categories: brick-and-mortar, online ventures, or service-based organizations.
Types of Start-up Costs
Start-up Expenses (One-time): Necessary equipment (cash registers, machinery, vehicles), permits and licenses, computer/technology equipment, down payment for office/store, initial inventory, initial office supplies, signage and office renovation, office furniture and fixtures
Start-up Expenses (Ongoing): Rent/mortgage, accounting services, taxes and legal services, business insurance, payroll and employee benefits, office supplies, website hosting, travel expenses, utilities (electricity, gas, water, phone, internet), marketing materials, ongoing inventory, loan/credit payments
Start-up Assets: Expenses for acquiring long-term assets — starting inventory, computers/technology, office equipment, office furniture, vehicles
Assets ≠ Expenses: expenses are deductible against income (reduce taxable income); assets are NOT deductible — separating them saves money on taxes
Cash: Starting cash balance = money from investments or loans minus money spent on expenses and assets
Capital Funding Strategies
Personal Investment: Individual invests and manages financial instruments — stocks, bonds, real estate; must create investment plans based on unique characteristics
Love Money: Money given by spouse, parents, friends, or other loved ones — "patient capital" repaid as the business's profits rise
Venture Capital: Wealthy investors, investment banks, and financial institutions provide financing to startups with long-term growth potential in exchange for equity/stocks
Loans: Most common form — a sum advanced by a lender (government agency, financial institution, or corporation); borrower agrees to finance charges, interest, and a repayment date
Angels: Wealthy individuals or retired executives who invest directly in small private businesses; bring experience and extensive networks; reserve the right to oversee management practices
Business Incubators: Future businesses share incubator spaces, technical, administrative, and logistical resources — common in biotechnology, IT, multimedia, and industrial technology
Crowdfunding: Business asks the general public for money — typically for equity; many people contribute small donations; contrasts with venture capital (small group, large amounts)
Grants and Subsidies: Grants are used for specific purposes and do not require repayment; subsidies are direct contributions, tax breaks, and government assistance to offset operating costs
📌 Examples
Love Money: A student borrows ₱20,000 from parents to start a printing business, promising to repay once profitable.
Venture Capital: A tech startup receives ₱5M from a VC firm in exchange for 20% equity in the company.
Crowdfunding: A Filipino filmmaker raises ₱300,000 via GoFundMe, rewarding backers with credits and merchandise.
Grant: DOLE-TUPAD and DSWD livelihood programs provide small capital grants to entrepreneurs without requiring repayment.
Asset vs. Expense: A ₱50,000 commercial oven is a fixed asset (not immediately deductible); a ₱500 bag of flour is an expense (deductible).
The Four Financial Statements Financial Statements
Depending on business size, preparing general-purpose financial statements can be simple or complex. Some statements need footnote disclosures; others can be presented without any. There are four key financial statements.
A. Income Statement
Shows a company's revenues, expenses, and net profit over a specific period of time
Helps business owners understand financial situation and make fast, well-informed decisions about expenses
Provides stakeholders, shareholders, and the owner insight into the business's financial state
Along with balance sheet and cash flow statement, provides data to calculate business taxes
Net income is carried forward to the Statement of Owner's Equity
B. Statement of Owner's Equity (Retained Earnings)
Shows changes in the owner's equity during a specific period — includes beginning/ending balance, net income/loss, additional investments, and withdrawals
Helps stakeholders understand how equity has changed and what factors contributed
Retained profits can be used for new machinery, R&D, or other growth activities
Used to evaluate financial health and performance; ending balance is carried to the Balance Sheet
C. Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position)
Shows a company's financial position at a specific point in time — a snapshot of assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity
Assets include cash, accounts receivable, inventory, and property; Liabilities include accounts payable, loans, obligations; Equity represents owner's investment and retained earnings
Shows financial health typically at end of fiscal year or end of month
Shows how much the business owes and how much assets are worth; helps monitor performance and assess ability to pay bills
D. Statement of Cash Flows
Shows how cash is generated from operating, investing, and financing activities over a specific period
Operating Activities: Cash from delivering regular goods or services — revenue and expenses
Investing Activities: Cash from purchasing or selling assets (real estate, vehicles, patents) using free cash — not debt
Financing Activities: Cash from both debt and equity financing
Reveals what phase a business is in — rapidly growing startup, mature company, transition, or decline
Cash flow impacts internal decisions: budgeting, hiring, and firing employees
Final cash balance on the Statement of Cash Flows must match the Cash shown on the Balance Sheet
📌 Examples
Income Statement: NetSolutions earned ₱7,500 in fees. Total expenses (wages, rent, supplies, utilities, misc) = ₱4,450. Net income = ₱3,050.
Owner's Equity: Beginning equity (Nov 1) = ₱0. Add: Net income ₱3,050. Less: Dividends ₱2,000. Ending balance (Nov 30) = ₱1,050.
Balance Sheet: Cash ₱5,900 + Supplies ₱550 + Land ₱20,000 = ₱26,450 (Assets). Accounts Payable ₱400 + Equity ₱26,050 = ₱26,450 (Liabilities + Equity). Balanced ✓
Cash Flow Red Flag: A company shows positive net income but negative operating cash flow — signals possible collection problems or earnings manipulation.
Elements of Financial Statements Accounting
Financial statements comprise five different categories: assets, liabilities, owner's equity, expenses, and revenue. These categories may vary depending on the size of the company.
A. Assets — Resources owned or controlled that can provide future economic benefits
Current Assets (converted to cash within one year): Cash (physical money), Accounts Receivable (unpaid amounts from customers), Inventory (stock of goods/materials), Prepaid Expense (expenses paid in advance)
Fixed (Non-current) Assets (life of more than one year, recorded as property, plant, and equipment):
Tangible Fixed Assets: Trucks, office furniture, machinery, buildings, land
Intangible Fixed Assets: Goodwill, patents, copyrights, trademarks, franchises
B. Liabilities — Obligations required by law to be paid
Current Liabilities (due within 12 months): Accounts Payable (unpaid amounts for goods/services), Salaries and Wages Payable (compensation to employees), Notes Payable (written promissory note), Mortgage Payable (property loan)
Non-current (Long-term) Liabilities (not due for more than 12 months): Customer Deposits, Lease Obligations, Pension Liabilities, Long-term Debt (loans with over 1 year maturity), Warranty Liabilities
C. Equity — Sum invested by owners plus retained earnings
Owner's Capital (amount contributed by owner), Owner's Withdrawals (money taken for personal use), Common Stocks (ownership shares), Retained Earnings (net income reinvested in the business)
D. Revenue — Measurement of total gross activity
Operating Revenue (from main operations): Sales, Professional Services, Rental Income, Commission Earned
Non-operating Revenue (from side activities): Dividends, Investment Income, Gains/Losses from Foreign Exchange, Sales of Assets
E. Expenses — Asset loses value to generate money
Operating Expenses: Cost of Sales, Utilities Expense, Purchases, Freight Expense, Advertising Expense, Depreciation Expense, Interest Expense, Rent Expense, Supplies Expense, License Fees and Taxes
Discretionary Expenses (nonessential): Company travel, Investments and innovations (R&D), Employee perks, Office improvements, Employee training
📌 Examples
Current Asset: A convenience store's cash register money (₱10,000) and customer IOUs are both current assets.
Intangible Asset: Jollibee's brand name (goodwill) and McDonald's golden arches (trademark) are valuable intangible fixed assets.
Non-operating Revenue: A manufacturing company earns ₱50,000 in bank interest — non-operating because it's not from their core manufacturing business.
Discretionary Expense: A company's annual team-building retreat in Batangas is non-essential spending — discretionary.
The Accounting Equation Accounting
According to Byjus (n.d.), the accounting equation is the fundamental element of the balance sheet and the primary principle of accounting. It aids businesses in preparing the Balance Sheet and determining whether total assets, liabilities, and owner's equity are equal.
Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity
Six Conclusions for a Balanced Equation
1. An increase in assets → corresponding increase in liability
2. An increase in assets → corresponding capital increase
3. An increase in one asset → opposite decrease in another asset
4. A decrease in assets → corresponding decrease in liability
5. A decrease in assets → corresponding decrease in capital
6. An increase in liability → corresponding decrease in capital
📌 Examples
Problem 1 — Owner invests ₱10,000: Assets = ₱10,000 | Liabilities = ₱0 | Equity = ₱10,000 → Balanced ✓
Problem 2 — Takes ₱10,000 loan to expand: Assets = ₱10,000 + ₱10,000 = ₱20,000 | Liabilities = ₱10,000 | Equity = ₱10,000 → ₱20,000 = ₱20,000 ✓
Problem 3 — Buys ₱4,000 supplies on credit: Assets = ₱24,000 | Liabilities = ₱14,000 | Equity = ₱10,000 → ₱24,000 = ₱24,000 ✓
Daily Life: Buying a ₱5,000 laptop on a credit card — laptop (asset) ↑₱5,000 AND credit card debt (liability) ↑₱5,000. Equation stays balanced.
